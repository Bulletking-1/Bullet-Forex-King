Smart Money Concepts

Learn how institutional traders approach the market with detailed explanations, visual illustrations, and strategies for practical application.

Liquidity Pools

Liquidity pools are price levels where a large cluster of pending stop-loss orders are positioned, usually around obvious highs and lows. Institutions target these pools to generate the volume they need for big moves.

Trading Tip: When price aggressively moves into a liquidity pool and immediately rejects, this is a signal that smart money has engineered a liquidity grab. Combine this with structure breaks for higher accuracy entries.

Order Blocks

An order block is the last bullish or bearish candle before a strong impulsive move. These zones represent areas where institutions placed large orders.

Trading Tip: After a sharp move, price often revisits the origin candle (order block) to mitigate pending institutional orders. Watch for confirmations like rejection wicks, engulfing candles, or lower timeframe break of structure.

Market Structure

Market structure is the backbone of technical analysis. It helps traders understand trend direction by analyzing highs and lows (HH, HL, LH, LL).

Trading Tip: Always align with the higher timeframe structure. For example, trade only longs when the daily timeframe is showing bullish structure (HH + HL), and look for CHoCH/BOS on lower timeframes for entry.

Supply & Demand Zones

These zones form when the market leaves behind sharp moves, indicating imbalances. They show where buyers or sellers were strongly in control.

Trading Tip: Entering at demand zones during bullish trends and at supply zones during bearish trends greatly improves risk-to-reward. Wait for price confirmation (candlestick patterns or structure shifts).

Fair Value Gaps (FVG)

A Fair Value Gap is a price imbalance caused by three candles: the first and third do not overlap, leaving a gap. Institutions tend to rebalance these inefficiencies.

Trading Tip: Mark FVGs on the 1H or 4H chart. When price retraces into these zones and rejects, it often continues in the original direction. FVGs also align well with order blocks.

Mitigation Blocks

A mitigation block occurs when price revisits a previous order block to close out remaining institutional orders. These levels often provide precise entries.

Trading Tip: Look for mitigation blocks when price returns to a previously violated order block. These setups are best traded with confluence such as BOS/CHoCH and liquidity sweeps.
